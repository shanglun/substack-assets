Regularization

Neural Networks · Chapter 7

How to stop a network from memorizing.

A neural network with enough parameters can fit anything — including the noise. Regularization is the discipline of giving a model just enough freedom to learn the pattern, but not so much that it learns the accidents.

Topic Overfitting · Penalties · Dropout · BatchNorm · Early Stopping

§ 1 / The ProblemThe student with photographic memory

Imagine a student preparing for an exam. Two strategies are available. The first student memorizes every practice problem verbatim — every typo, every coincidence, every detail. The second student learns the underlying ideas and is willing to be a little wrong on the practice set if it means being right on the real exam.

A neural network with millions of parameters is, by default, the first student. It has enough capacity to bend itself into whatever shape perfectly explains the training data, noise and all. We call this overfitting. The model achieves a tiny training error and a terrible test error — it learned the practice set, not the subject.

Deep neural networks are prone to overfitting because they try to capture complex patterns in the data — and in doing so, they capture the noise. — The core motivation for everything that follows

We then fit a flexible 9-degree polynomial to those samples. § 2 / The Penalty ApproachCharge a tax on complexity

The most direct way to discourage a model from getting too complicated is to charge it for being complicated. We extend the loss function so that the model is now optimizing two things at once: fitting the data, and keeping its weights small.

Loss = Data Error + λ · (size of weights) The two-term loss that powers L1 and L2

The hyperparameter λ (lambda) controls how seriously we take the penalty. Small λ means "I mostly care about fitting the data, just nudge me toward smaller weights." Large λ means "I'd rather have a flat-out wrong model than one with big weights."

The question becomes: what counts as the size of the weights? There are two famous answers.

L2 (Ridge): penalize the squared weights

Penalty = w12 + w22 + w32 + … Sum of squares — the L2 norm

Squaring punishes big weights disproportionately. A weight of 10 costs 100; a weight of 1 costs only 1. So L2 has a strong opinion about outliers in weight-space and barely cares about the small fry. The result is that every weight gets gently shrunk, but rarely all the way to zero. Picture a network of springs attached to every weight, pulling each one toward zero with a force that grows with how stretched the spring is.

L1 (Lasso): penalize the absolute weights

Penalty = |w1| + |w2| + |w3| + … Sum of absolute values — the L1 norm

With absolute value, the marginal cost of a weight is constant. Pushing a weight from 0.01 to 0.02 costs the same as pushing it from 5.01 to 5.02. The penalty doesn't pity small weights the way L2 does — even tiny weights get squeezed.

The consequence is dramatic: L1 will happily push weights all the way to exactly zero if their contribution to fitting the data is smaller than λ. The model becomes sparse. It effectively says, "I don't need these features at all" and deletes them.

L2 / Ridge

Many small weights. Smooth. Like turning every dial down a little. Good when you believe all features matter a bit.

L1 / Lasso

A few non-zero weights, the rest zero. Like a binary selector. Good when you believe only some features actually matter.

The geometric picture

A pretty piece of intuition: think of the penalty as a "budget" you have to live within. With L2, that budget forms a circle (round). With L1, it forms a diamond (with sharp corners). When the optimizer is trying to balance fitting the data against staying inside the budget, it tends to land where the data's contours first touch the budget shape. A circle is most likely to be touched on a curve where all coordinates are non-zero. A diamond is most likely to be touched at a corner — and the corners lie exactly on the axes, where some coordinates are zero. That's the geometric reason L1 produces sparsity.

Go back to the widget and switch between L2 and L1 at moderate λ. Look at the coefficient bars on the right. With L2, you'll see all ten bars are short. With L1, many of them are flat — exactly zero.

§ 3 / The Noise ApproachDropout: train an army of mediocre networks

Penalties shrink weights. Dropout takes a stranger and more interesting approach: at every step of training, it randomly turns off a fraction of the neurons. Each neuron is independently set to zero with probability p (the dropout rate). The remaining neurons handle the forward and backward pass alone, then everyone wakes up again, and on the next batch a different random subset gets dropped.

Dropout · drag the slider to change drop rate

Why does randomly destroying parts of your network help? Because the surviving neurons can never count on any specific other neuron being there. They can't form little cliques of co-dependency where, say, neuron A only fires correctly because neurons B and C are always around to provide context. Every neuron has to learn a feature that's robust on its own, useful even when half its neighbors are silent.

There's a second, deeper interpretation. Each random pattern of dropouts defines a different "subnetwork." Training with dropout is, secretly, training a huge ensemble of these subnetworks simultaneously, all sharing the same weights. At test time we use the full network with the weights scaled down (multiplied by 1/(1-p) to keep the expected output the same), which approximates the average of that ensemble. You get the variance-reducing benefit of ensembling without the cost of training many networks.

A fully connected layer occupies most of the parameters, and hence, neurons develop co-dependency amongst each other during training which curbs the individual power of each neuron — leading to overfitting. This is handled by using Dropout. — Why dropout exists, in one paragraph

§ 4 / The StabilizerBatch Normalization: tame the river

Batch Normalization is a slightly different beast. It's not primarily a regularizer; its main job is to stabilize training. But it has a real regularizing side-effect, so it lives in this neighborhood.

Inside a deep network, the inputs to each layer keep shifting around as the previous layers update their weights. One layer is trying to learn a moving target. Batch norm fixes this by explicitly normalizing the inputs to each layer — subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation, computed over the current mini-batch. The downstream layer now sees inputs that are always centered at zero with unit variance.

Batch Normalization · before vs. after

The benefits compound: gradients flow more reliably, you can use larger learning rates, training converges faster. The regularization effect is a bonus — since the normalization statistics are computed per mini-batch, they're noisy, and that noise wiggles each example's activations in a slightly different way every epoch. It's a very mild dropout-flavored effect.

§ 5 / The Lazy ApproachEarly stopping: just don't train so long

Here's the simplest regularizer in the world, and it works embarrassingly well: watch the validation loss, and stop training the moment it stops improving.

During training, the loss on the training set will fall monotonically (give or take some noise). The loss on a held-out validation set will fall too — at first. But at some point, the model starts memorizing training-set quirks that don't generalize, and validation loss starts climbing back up while training loss keeps falling. The gap between the two curves is the visible signature of overfitting.

The fix: just stop at the bottom of the validation curve. No extra parameters, no special math — just a checkpoint of the model whenever the validation loss hits a new minimum, and a patience counter to decide when to give up. It's so easy that it's almost always worth doing, even when you're already using other regularizers.

§ 6 / In PracticeYou usually use several at once

These techniques aren't in competition. A typical modern training recipe layers them: weight decay (a name for L2) on the optimizer, dropout in the fully-connected layers, batch (or layer) normalization between layers, and early stopping with patience based on validation loss. Each addresses a slightly different failure mode.

The right amount of each is a hyperparameter you tune by watching the training/validation gap. If your validation loss is plateauing while training loss continues to drop, you're overfitting and need more regularization. If both losses are stuck high together, you're underfitting and need less regularization — or a bigger model, or more data.

Regularization is a dial, not a switch. The whole game is finding the right amount — enough to suppress noise, not so much that you suppress signal. — The whole subject in one sentence

§ 7 / RecapThe map of techniques

  • L2 / Ridge / Weight decay — penalize squared weights. Smooths everything.
  • L1 / Lasso — penalize absolute weights. Drives many to exactly zero (sparsity).
  • Dropout — randomly zero out neurons during training. Forces robustness, approximates ensembling.
  • Batch Normalization — normalize each layer's inputs. Stabilizes training; mildly regularizes.
  • Early Stopping — quit while you're ahead. Watch validation loss; stop at its minimum.
  • (Bonus) Data augmentation — randomly perturb training examples (rotations, crops, etc). Effectively gives the model more data.
