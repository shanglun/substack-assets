01 · The Setup

You're a hiker. In thick fog.

You're standing somewhere on a hillside. You can't see five feet ahead. But you can feel which way is downhill beneath your boots. How do you find the valley floor?

You take a small step in the steepest downhill direction. Then you stop, re-check the slope under your feet, take another step. Repeat until the ground feels flat. You've just performed gradient descent.

Training a neural network is the same problem in disguise. The "landscape" is the model's error. The "position" is its weights. The algorithm has no map — only a sense of which way is downhill.
02 · The Landscape

The loss function is the landscape.

Every setting of a model's weights produces some amount of error on the training data. Plot that error against the weights and you get a surface — the loss landscape.

Low spots = good weights (small error, accurate predictions). High spots = bad weights. Our job is to find a low spot.

With one weight, the landscape is a curve. With two, a surface. With a million weights — like a real neural network — it's a million-dimensional terrain we can't draw but can still walk across, one step at a time.

03 · The Gradient

The gradient is just "which way is up."

At any point on the landscape, the gradient is an arrow that points in the direction of steepest ascent, and whose length tells you how steep that ascent is.

Drag the dot in the panel on the right. Watch the tangent line tilt. That tilt — its sign and its magnitude — is the gradient at that point.

  • · On the left wall, slope tilts down-right → gradient is negative
  • · On the right wall, slope tilts up-right → gradient is positive
  • · At the bottom, slope is flat → gradient is zero
Zero gradient means flat ground. That's our signal we've arrived — there's no "downhill" left to walk.

04 · The Update Rule

One line of math, read it slowly.

wnew = wold − α · ∇L(w)
new position = old position − step size × slope

Why subtract?

The gradient ∇L points uphill. We want to go downhill. So we go the opposite way — hence the minus sign. If the slope under your feet tilts up to the right (positive gradient), you step left. If it tilts up to the left (negative gradient), you step right.

What's α?

The learning rate — how big a step you take. A small α means tiny, careful shuffles. A large α means giant leaps. We'll see in a moment why this single number is the difference between training a model successfully and watching it explode.

06 · The Learning Rate

Three settings. Three fates.

The learning rate is the most consequential hyperparameter in deep learning. Below, three identical problems run side-by-side with three different values of α. They all start at the same spot.

α = 0.02

too small
Crawls forever. Progress is real, but glacial. Training would burn through compute before it reaches the bottom.

α = 0.3

just right
Goldilocks zone. Confident strides, then naturally smaller steps as it nears the minimum. Settles into the bottom cleanly.

α = 1.05

too large
Overshoots and diverges. Each step jumps clear over the valley to the opposite wall — higher than where it started. Loss explodes.
In practice, picking α is half art, half experiment. Modern training uses schedules (start big, shrink over time) and adaptive optimizers like Adam that adjust α per-weight. But the intuition is what you just saw: too timid wastes time, too bold detonates.
07 · More Than One Weight

Real models live in many dimensions.

A neural network doesn't have one weight, it has thousands or billions. The algorithm doesn't change — at each step, it computes the gradient (now a vector pointing downhill in all dimensions at once) and takes a step against it. Here's the 2D version, viewed from above.

Notice how the ball doesn't always head straight for the bottom. In valleys that are steeper one way than the other (called ill-conditioned landscapes), gradient descent zig-zags. This is why advanced optimizers exist — to smooth out that wobble.
08 · The Catch

Sometimes you find a valley, but not the valley.

Real loss landscapes aren't smooth bowls. They have bumps, ridges, and multiple valleys at different depths. Gradient descent only knows local information — it walks downhill from wherever it starts. So where you start can determine where you end up.

The good news

In very high dimensions (which is where real neural networks live), it turns out that bad local minima are surprisingly rare. Most of the "stuck points" turn out to be saddle points — places that are downhill in some directions and uphill in others. A little noise in the gradient (more on that in a moment) is usually enough to escape them.

The not-so-good news

Picking a good starting point still matters. So does adding momentum, using adaptive learning rates, and — the most underrated trick — sheer luck. Modern training pipelines combine clever initialization, smart optimizers, and lots of experimentation to push the ball toward a deep valley.

09 · The Three Flavors

How much data per step?

Every step requires computing a gradient on some training data. The question is: how much data? That single choice gives us the three variants every textbook covers — and a tradeoff between accuracy, speed, and noise.

Variant 01
Batch Gradient Descent

Compute the gradient using every single training example before taking one step. The thoughtful, careful walker.

  • + Stable, smooth path to a minimum.
  • + Each step is the "true" gradient.
  • − Painfully slow on big datasets.
  • − Tends to get stuck in shallow basins.
Variant 02
Stochastic GD (SGD)

Compute the gradient using one training example at a time, then step. Fast, jittery, and surprisingly effective.

  • + Updates immediately — fast feedback.
  • + Noise helps escape shallow minima.
  • − The path wobbles a lot.
  • − Sensitive to outliers and bad samples.
Variant 03 · The Default
Mini-batch GD

A small batch (32, 64, 128 examples) per step. Best of both worlds — and what virtually every modern deep learning system actually uses.

  • + Stable enough, fast enough.
  • + Plays beautifully with GPU parallelism.
  • − Batch size becomes one more knob to tune.
  • ~ What "SGD" usually means in practice.

10 · The Bigger Picture

From parabolas to GPT-scale models.

The ball you've been watching roll down toy curves? It's the same algorithm — at staggering scale — that trains every neural network from image classifiers to language models.

In a deep network, the "landscape" lives in the space of all weights (millions, often billions, of dimensions). The gradient is computed by an algorithm called backpropagation, which efficiently calculates how each weight contributes to the final error.

Then for each weight, the same update rule fires: w ← w − α · ∇L. The same patient, blind, downhill walk. Just running across a landscape no human can visualize, for billions of steps.

What changes in practice are the embellishments: momentum (keep moving in directions that have been consistently downhill), adaptive learning rates per weight (Adam, RMSprop), gradient clipping (don't let any step get too big), and learning rate schedules (start bold, get careful).

But underneath, it's the algorithm you just watched in the playground. A ball on a hill, taking one step at a time, looking only at its feet.

Every time a model gets smarter — a chatbot improves, a medical scanner gets better at spotting tumors, a translation gets more fluent — it's because somewhere a gradient was computed, and a weight took a tiny step downhill.
